# Instructional

## Purpose
Voice category covering instructional and educational voice styles for audio production prompting.

## Status: draft

## Delivery Style

Instructional content prioritises comprehension above all else. The voice's job is to get the information into the listener's head cleanly — not to entertain, not to inspire, not to persuade. Every delivery choice should serve that goal.

Deliberate pacing gives listeners time to follow along. When the instruction requires action — clicking a setting, navigating a menu, adjusting a parameter — the listener needs a moment between steps. Pacing that runs ahead of the action is the failure mode in this category. Slower than conversational is correct.

Consistent register means the voice does not distract. When register is stable and expressiveness variance is low, the voice recedes behind the content. The listener stops hearing the voice and starts absorbing the instruction. This is the goal. A voice that draws attention to itself — through pitch swings, energy shifts, or character — competes with comprehension.

Warm authority sits in the middle of the warmth-vs-authority spectrum: neither cold and clinical (which creates distance and distrust) nor too casual (which undermines credibility). The tone of a trusted teacher. Confident enough to be believed, warm enough to be approachable. This is the most broadly useful professional voice register.

Style set to 0.0 is a deliberate choice. Style amplification adds expressiveness, which adds variability, which adds distraction. In instructional content, added expressiveness is noise. The text should carry the instruction; the voice carries it cleanly.

## Example Lines

1. "Here's what you're going to do."
2. "Start by opening the settings menu."
3. "This step is the one people usually skip."
4. "Take your time here — it matters."
5. "When it looks like this, you're ready to move on."

## Settings Guidance

**Stability:** Higher (0.65). Consistency across long-form instruction is the primary requirement. A stable voice does not shift character between steps, does not introduce unexpected variation, and does not draw the listener's attention. Higher stability produces the steady, reliable baseline this category needs.

**Style:** None (0.0). Zero style amplification. This is the only category in the set that benefits from a hard zero here. Added expressiveness is a distraction. The script carries the instruction; the voice delivers it without interpretation.

**Expressiveness:** Lower variance. Not flat — the voice should still sound human and engaged — but the range of variation should be narrow. Consistent register across the delivery means the listener can focus on the content, not the voice character.

**Pacing:** Deliberately slower than conversational baseline. Between steps, between key terms, after instructions that require action. Short sentences. Full stops that actually stop. Do not rush. Each phrase should be fully closed before the next opens.

**Energy:** Moderate. Present and engaged, not elevated. The voice signals that the content matters without urgency or hype. Confident delivery at moderate energy reads as competent authority — which is exactly the instructional register.

**Warmth vs authority:** Blended centre. Neither warmth-dominant nor authority-dominant. This is the trusted teacher zone: approachable enough to be comfortable, authoritative enough to be trusted. Mid-register voice selection supports this blend naturally.

**Speaker boost:** On. Comprehension depends on clear enunciation. Speaker boost supports distinct pronunciation, particularly for technical terms, product names, or multi-step instructions where mishearing is a real failure mode.
