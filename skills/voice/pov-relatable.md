# POV Relatable

## Purpose
Voice category covering POV and relatable personal narrative voice styles for audio production prompting.

## Status: draft

## Delivery Style

POV relatable is the most intimate category in the set. The listener should feel like the speaker is talking directly to them — not at them. The difference is register and energy: warmth over authority, low-to-mid energy, and first-person framing. The listener is inside the speaker's experience, not being addressed from the outside.

Low energy is deliberate. Calm reads as approachable, not uninterested. High energy in this format feels like performance — like the speaker is trying to be relatable rather than actually being relatable. Measured, unhurried delivery signals genuine reflection, not presentation.

**Breath and micro-pauses are core delivery tools in this category, not optional.** Breath humanises AI voice — the absence of audible breath is one of the most reliable synthetic tells. In POV relatable content, that tell is especially damaging because the whole format depends on the listener believing the speaker is a real person reflecting in real time. Micro-pauses between phrases signal that the speaker is thinking, not reciting. Breath marks the beginning of a new thought.

Write breath beats into the script using commas, dashes, and natural inhale points. One breath every two to three sentences is a usable baseline. Do not write long, unbroken clauses in POV content — every long clause is a missed breath cue.

First-person framing and conversational register keep the listener inside the experience. The warmth-over-authority calibration means the voice errs toward intimate and approachable rather than credible and direct. This is not a content type that needs to convince — it needs to connect.

## Example Lines

1. "I used to think I had it figured out."
2. "Nobody told me it would feel like this."
3. "Honestly? I cried. And then I kept going."
4. "This is the part no one talks about."
5. "I'm not the same person I was last year. And that's okay."

## Settings Guidance

**Stability:** Mid-range (0.5). Enough variation to feel human and reflective, but not so low that the voice sounds unstable or inconsistent. POV content often runs long and voice consistency matters across the full clip — a too-low stability will make the voice feel different across sentences.

**Similarity boost:** Higher (0.8). Consistent voice identity is a priority in this category. The listener needs to believe this is the same person throughout — higher similarity boost keeps the character anchored.

**Style:** Moderate (0.2). Light emotional colouring only. Let the text and delivery carry the emotion — the script is doing the intimacy work. Added expressiveness risks turning genuine reflection into performance. Keep it subtle.

**Breath:** Write it in at the script level. Commas, dashes, short sentences, ellipses — these are breath instructions. The model will produce more natural breath beats when the script forces natural pause points. Do not rely on settings to add breath; it must be scripted.

**Pacing:** Unhurried. Slightly slower than conversational baseline. Each sentence should land before the next begins. Rushing signals anxiety or performance; slow delivery signals presence and reflection.

**Energy:** Low-to-mid. Calm, subdued, genuinely present. Not flat — engaged — but not pressing toward the listener. The voice should feel like it is sharing, not pitching.

**Warmth vs authority:** Warmth-dominant. This is the most warmth-leaning category in the set. Intimate, approachable, soft-edged. Authority should be minimal — this is a personal narrative, not a presentation.
